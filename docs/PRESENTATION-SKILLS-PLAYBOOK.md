# Tikal Presentation Skills Playbook

> **Purpose**: This handbook helps you prepare and present a lecture according to the best practices and methodologies assembled at Tikal.

---

## The Triangle of Alliances

The triangle has 3 vertices representing the 3 major parts of a lecture:

- **The Lecturer** — the person who prepares and presents the lecture.
- **The Audience** — the group of people that are the target of the lecture.
- **The Content** — the topic and material that the lecture is about.

The 3 edges represent the "alliances" between each part:

1. When a lecturer **prepares** a lecture — he forges an alliance with the **content**, setting all focus on research, formalization, and presentation of the material.
2. When the lecturer **meets the audience** — he must break the alliance with the content and forge a new one with the **audience**. He should pay full attention to the audience, even adjusting the content on the fly to match the audience and the presentation.
3. After the lecture is over — the result should be that the **audience forges an alliance with the content**, taking with them the knowledge, using it, and passing it on.

## 1. The Lecturer

### Trust

In order to accept information from a talk, the audience needs to trust the lecturer as a "domain expert." Ways to establish this trust:

- Reputation (known in this field, or audience has seen previous lectures)
- Presented by someone the audience trusts (friend's-friend rule)
- Brief credentials / background / accomplishments shared during the opening
- Answering questions in a way that establishes domain expertise
- Self-confidence and passion naturally generate trust

### Authenticity

- Content should be original, unfamiliar, and refreshing. If borrowing from specific sources, credit them.
- It's okay not to know an answer — say: *"I'm not sure, but email me and I'll get back to you."* This actually builds trust.
- If you say something inaccurate and someone calls it out: thank them, acknowledge the mistake, correct it, and move on. Once is recoverable; multiple times signals insufficient preparation.
- If you're inexperienced with audiences: share that openly and ask for help throughout — most listeners will respect it and hold back criticism.
- Experienced speakers learn to defuse awkward situations; the very best turn them into a joke.

### Body Language

- Avoid scratching head/neck or standing with hands joined — these signal low confidence.
- Use **open positions**: chest directed at the audience, arms/hands open (or behind your back).
- Keep eyes looking at the crowd; make frequent, intentional eye contact with individuals.
- If unsure where to look: pick a few virtual spots and rotate between them.
- **Smile.**

### Techniques

- **Pause instead of filler words**: Replace "ah…" and "mm…" with a short pause mid-sentence (e.g., *"I wanted to — tell you about…"*). This gives planning time and creates tension that re-engages the audience.
- **Self-introduction as a story, not bullets**:
  - ❌ Bullet approach: *"Hi, I'm Nir. I have a CS degree. 16 years in the industry. Backend engineer."*
  - ✅ Story approach: *"Hi, my name is Nir and I've been a backend engineer, like most of you, for 16 years. I worked at two startups as well as a small company you've probably never heard of called Netflix…"*
- **Vary your tone** — a monotone voice will put the audience to sleep.
- **Move around** — movement attracts focus and keeps your blood flowing, helping you stay sharp.
- **Speak with your hands** — intentional gestures (drawing shapes like circles/squares that correlate with the content) help track tension and convey meaning.
- **Ask the audience questions**:
  - Teasing / curiosity questions
  - "Opening questions" before starting a new section
  - "Closing questions" to verify understanding
- **Prepare pre-defined phrases** for common situations:
  - Unknown answers: *"As far as I know…"* / *"I haven't researched that topic completely"* / *"Great question — I'll need to look into it"*
  - Connection/transition phrases: *"Well…"* / *"Check this out…"* / *"Here's the deal…"* / *"If so…"* / *"Though…"*
- **Rehearse, record, and listen back** — replay recordings during commutes or walks to identify improvements.
- **Don't turn your back to the audience** — use a pointer or an additional monitor instead.
- **Use metaphors and real-world analogies** to keep the audience engaged.
- **Use speaker notes** — find a method you're comfortable with and practice using it.
- **Avoid overusing "I"** (I did, I think, I've been…)
- **Close all notification apps** (WhatsApp, email, Slack) before presenting. A personal notification mid-lecture is embarrassing and distracting.

## 2. The Content

### Building a Presentation from Scratch

1. Create a list of topics to cover.
2. Elaborate on each topic in bullets — casual brainstorm, capture all ideas.
3. Copy the list into a presentation — each topic in one or more slides with its bullets.
4. If a topic is too large for one slide, split it into a section with sub-slides.
5. Go through each slide, extending, rephrasing, and correcting until all material is covered.
6. Give critical self-feedback:
   - Are the slides in an ideal order?
   - Do we cover everything needed?
   - Do we cover too much?
7. **Prettify**: Replace text-heavy slides with diagrams, images, tables, comparisons, quotes, or key words. Move the rest to "speaker notes."
8. Add transitions & animations (optional) to enhance flow.
9. Review, correct, and iterate until it feels ready — this is an iterative process.

> When reusing an old presentation, always do at least one full iteration of updates and improvements.

---

### Topic Selection

#### Focused but Not Too Niche
- **Too broad**: insufficient depth, clarity suffers, audience loses track.
- **Too niche**: irrelevant to the majority of attendees.
- **Balance is the key.**

#### Relevancy
Choose topics that intersect two factors:
- **Current tech trends** — innovative, emerging, or controversial; or a new interpretation of popular technology. Use tools like Tikal's Tech Radar, NPM download counts, GitHub stars, Stack Overflow annual surveys.
- **Your specific audience** — what is their seniority, domain, and current needs?

---

### From Subject to Story

Stories engage far better than a collection of facts — the audience always wants to know *"what happens next."*

**Story recipe:**
1. **Disruptive opening** — break the audience's perception of reality; make them feel just slightly uncertain about something they thought they knew.
2. **Choose a hero** — can be you, another real person, or a fictional character.
3. **The hero encounters a problem** — important and relevant to the lecture topic.
4. **The naive solution** — the hero tries a simple fix and fails (can repeat multiple times).
5. **The inspiration** — the hero recognises the flaw in the naive approach and finds insight.
6. **The solution** — the hero solves the problem correctly and succeeds.
7. **The aftermath** — revisit the disruptive opening; show how the hero's journey changed the picture.

---

### Slides

> **Core rule**: Slides *support* the talk — they are NOT the talk. Plan the talk first (as if there are no slides at all). Only then think about slides.

**Best practices:**
- Limit text and bullet points — present information in small chunks.
- A picture is worth a thousand words; a funny/interesting/intriguing picture is worth even more.
- Use high-resolution images.
- Be consistent with fonts, headers, and title sizes across all slides.
- Keep the number of objects per slide ≤ 6 (our brain counts up to 6 instantly; 7+ causes a delay). Avoid redundant slide numbers and logos on every slide.
- Keep speaker notes in the notes area, not visible on screen.
- **Use a dark background** with bright text — high contrast keeps the audience's eyes on the screen when needed, and lets you reclaim attention when you switch to a blank slide.
- **10-20-30 rule**: ~10 slides, ~20 minutes, font size ≥ 30.
- Insert a **blank slide** when you want all focus on you.

---

### Code Display

- Live coding is high-risk — always have a "Plan B" (code in slides or a pre-recorded clip).
- Typing and talking simultaneously is hard. Consider using a **"pilot"** to type while you talk. If you use a pilot — rehearse together.
- Techniques to guide the audience's attention to specific code:
  - **Animations** to reveal or highlight specific lines.
  - **Color/highlight** the relevant portion.
  - **Laser pointer.**
  - **Duplicate the slide** with different sections highlighted for each discussion point.

## 3. The Audience

- **Know your audience**: level of seniority, prerequisites, domain expertise.
- Adjust content accordingly (e.g., a Kubernetes talk differs significantly for frontend vs. backend developers).
- **Be more loyal to the audience than to the content**: if a section you prepared for "rookie level" turns out to be below the audience's actual level — skip it and move on.

## Lecturing Remotely

Remote delivery (Zoom, etc.) is significantly more challenging and requires deliberate adaptation.

### Key Challenges
- Equipment setup (camera, microphone, monitor position)
- Lower audience attention span

### How to Overcome

- **Two monitors are mandatory** — one for your audience's faces, one for your slides/code.
- **Clear audio** — no echo or feedback. Fancy mic optional; clarity is not.
- **Camera in front of you** — audience should see you face-on, not in profile.
- **Build interactive elements** throughout to maintain engagement.
- **Camera elevated** so it looks slightly down at you (more flattering angle). Sit, don't stand, to stay close to the camera.
- **Engage constantly** — ask questions, solicit answers, keep participants active.
- **Encourage cameras on** — a participant with camera on feels more accountable.
- **Use breakout rooms** for extended coding exercises or multi-topic discussions.
- **Virtual background or clean physical background** — nothing behind you should steal focus.
- **Notifications off** — especially personal channels (WhatsApp, private Slack).
- If a lecture was designed for one format (remote/frontal) and switches to the other — rehearse both.

## Addendum: Workshops

### Checkup
- The Lecture Advisor (LA) should verify exercises live with the lecturer (content correctness + "does everything work?").
- Recommended to have multiple reviewers assess:
  - Are topics explained clearly?
  - Are additional resources needed?
  - Are exercises clear, and appropriately levelled?
  - How long does the full workshop take?
- Lecturer should account for different environments and platforms.

### Preparation
- Share prerequisites before the lecture: reading, package installs, environment setup.
- Write an instructions document and attach it to the invitation.
- Include a self-check step (e.g., `--version` to confirm correct installs).
- Send multiple reminders; last reminder: **one day before**.

### Approach

| Approach | Focus | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Practical / Challenges** | Hands-on problem solving; minimal theory | Audience learns by doing; production-ready after |
| **Theoretical / Walkthrough** | Architecture, design, motivation; step-by-step guide | Audience understands the "how/why"; not immediately production-ready |

> In short: **Solving the problem** (Practical) vs **getting to know the tool** (Theoretical).

### Structure
- **Lecture portion**: More emphasis in the theoretical approach (in-depth); lighter in the practical approach (just enough background to solve challenges).
- **Hands-on portion**: More emphasis in the practical approach (solving real challenges); in the theoretical approach this is a guided walkthrough.
- The two portions can be intertwined.

### Exercises
- Communicate your approach (theoretical/practical) upfront to set expectations.
- Split audience into smaller groups where possible (breakout rooms in Zoom).
- **Build up gradually** — start easy, increase difficulty progressively.
- Practical approach: provide solutions for the first 2–3 items, then avoid spoon-feeding.
- Share final answers at the end of the session or in a follow-up email.
- For "one step on top of the other" workshops: create separate branches (questions-only vs. solutions) so attendees can continue even if stuck.
- Ground exercises in **real-world problems with small but complete examples** — attendees should be able to take them home as references.
- Consider using tools like **Codelab**.

## Definition of Quality by Lecture Rank

| Rank | Venue | Quality Bar | Advisor Sessions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lightning Talk** | Casual / internal | Raw, minimal polish | Usually none |
| **Group Lecture** | Standard Tikal internal lecture | Good level, covers most of this handbook | 1 (more optional) |
| **Meetup** | External audience / community | Polished; represents Tikal publicly | ≥ 1 (more strongly recommended) |
| **Convention** | Large external audience | Pristine condition | Multiple mandatory; cycles until final sign-off from Lior |
