# Presentation Skills Playbook

This handbook's purpose is to help you prepare and present a lecture according to the best practices and methodologies we have assembled at Tikal.

---

## The Triangle of Alliances

The triangle above has 3 vertices that represent the 3 major parts of a lecture:

- **The lecturer** — the person who prepares and presents the lecture.
- **The audience** — the group of people that are the target of the lecture.
- **The content** — the topic and material that the lecture is about.

The 3 edges of the triangle represent the "alliances" between each of the parts:

When a lecturer prepares a lecture — they forge an alliance with the content, setting all their focus in the research, formalization and presentation of the material.

Then, when the lecturer meets the audience, they need to break the alliance they had with the content and forge a new one — with the audience. They should pay full attention to the audience, even adjusting the content if needed on the fly — to match the audience and the presentation.

After the lecture is over, the result should be that the audience forges an alliance with the content, taking with them the knowledge, using it and passing it over.

This handbook will discuss the three parts of the lecture (lecturer, audience and content) and the relations between them.

## The Lecturer

### Trust

In order to accept the information from a talk, we need to trust the lecturer as a "domain expert". There are a few ways in which a person can get this kind of trust:

- Reputation (known in this field, or saw previous lectures of)
- Presented to us by someone we trust (friend's friend rule)
- Hear a bit about their credentials/background/accomplishments (usually during the opening of a lecture)
- Have them answer a question/s in a way that establishes them as domain experts

We tend to trust people with self confidence & passion.

### Authenticity

The content should be original, unfamiliar and refreshing. If the presenter "borrowed" information from specific sources, it should be mentioned and credited.

It's okay to not know an answer to a question, it actually establishes trust when you respond with: "I'm not sure about the answer to this question but you can email me and I'll get back to you"

It's not ideal when a lecturer says something inaccurate or obsolete, and if someone from the crowd calls it out it's important to acknowledge it and correct your mistake! A good way to do it is to thank the person who called you out for the correction, admit your mistake and move on. If it happens once during a session it's not that bad, if it happens more than once it means that the work wasn't done thoroughly enough and needs some rework!

People who are afraid to stand in front of an audience and don't have much experience: it's perfectly fine to share it with the audience and ask them for help throughout the talk and for feedback afterwards. Say that you want their help in order to improve and do better and most chances that the "smartass" in the croud will think twice before they start making sarcastic comments and such

People that have more experience with audience learn the skills of how to defuse awkward situations, the ones that are really good at it will even turn it into a joke

### Body language

Consider that your audience has some knowledge for body languages etiquette, and can identify when you aren't authentic or unconfident with this position.

Try to avoid scratching your head and your neck stand with your hands joined, this relays an unconfident behaviour. Instead try to use "open positions", in which your chest is directed towards the audience and your arms and hands are open (or even behind your back at times).

Keep your eyes looking to the crowd or make frequent eye contact with people in the audience. (not straight look may be considered as none trust )

If you don't know where to look at, pick some virtual spots and follow these with your eyes frequently.

Smile!

### Techniques

Instead of saying "ah.." and "mm…" when you're stuck, make it a habit to make a short pause in the middle of every sentence, especially in unexpected "places", e.g. "I wanted to (short pause) tell you about …" by having these short pauses you give yourselves extra time to plan ahead what you want to say and how, and as a bonus, these pauses create tension which brings the audience's focus back to you

In the beginning of the lecture, when you introduce yourself, try to do it as a "short story" instead of "bullets". Most people introduce themselves using the Bullet approach:

- Hi, My name is Nir
- I have a degree in CS and a second degree in Mathematics
- I've been working in the industry for 16 years
- I'm a backend engineer

Instead, try to tell it as a short paragraph (Not written, spoken as a "very short story"):

Hi, my name is Nir and I've been working as a backend engineer, like most of you, for the past 16 years. I worked for two startups as well as a small company you probably never heard of called Netflix…

If your tone/voice doesn't change — the audience will fall asleep

Movement helps to attract focus as well as helps your blood flow which helps to keep you "sharp" during the lecture

It's good to "speak with your hands" because movement helps track tension, it's even better when you're aware of what your hands are doing: Yael Man recommends doing imaginary "shapes" with your hands (which should correlate with the content of course), she provides a few examples about "drawing" circles, squares and etc on her talk

**Ask the audience questions:**

- Teasing questions
- "Opening questions" before you talk about something
- "Closing questions": verify that they understood the last "section

**Prepare pre-defined phrases for situations:**

When asked questions which you don't know the answer to such as:

- As far as I know… - למיטב ידיעתי
- I haven't researched that topic completely - לא חקרתי את הנושא הזה
- It's a good question, I will have to make some research on it - שאלה טובה, אצטרך לבדוק את העניין

Get a stack of connection phrases such as:

- Well - ובכן
- Check this out, now pay attention to this - שימו לב
- Here's the deal… (stolen from Biden)
- If so - אם כן
- Though - אם כי

Make rehearsals, record yourself and listen to your rehearsals in your free time

Try to have the lecture "recorded" in your head.. Listening to your previous lectures may give you ideas on what can be improved in next lectures. You can listen to your own lectures in your free time like:

- When driving
- Walking in the park

Try not to turn your back to the audience too much, use a pointer or use an additional monitor screen during the lecture.

Use metaphors and real world similarities, this may help to keep the audience focused and interested

Most of us need notes otherwise we'll forget certain points we wanted to discuss or the order in which we wanted to discuss them, using notes is not trivial, there are multiple different options for speaker's notes, find one you're comfortable with and practice using it

Try to avoid saying I (אני), too much..

- I did, I think, I've been..

Close any notifications apps which may popup during your lecture such as WhatsApp or email. Personal notifications during lecture may be embracing

## The Content

### Slides — creating a presentation from scratch

First, create a list of the topics you want to cover on your presentation.

Then, Elaborate on each topic in bullets. Do it casually, brainstorm about it. The idea is to write down all your ideas.

Copy paste your list into a presentation — each subject in a slide or more, with its relevant bullets.

If a subject is "too big" for one slide- think how you split it into smaller subjects. It can also be a complete section of the presentation.

Now, when you have a skeleton of the presentation — go through each slide and add more to the content, extending, rephrasing and correcting it, until you feel you covered all the material.

Try to give critical feedback to yourself:

- Are the slides in an ideal order?
- Do we cover all that needs to be covered?
- Do we cover too much?

It's time to "prettify" your presentation. Go to each slide and find a better way to present its content — a diagram, an image, a table, a comparison, a quote, or just single key words. Put the rest of the content of the slide as "speaker notes".

Transitions & Animations (optional)- go through each slide and decide if you should transit to it with an animation. Animations can also be used inside the slides to enhance the experience.

Review your presentation and make final corrections and adjustments until it feels ready.

It's an iterative process, which can be done over and over again until you feel that it's "ready" or at least "ready enough"

When we want to reuse a presentation we already created a while ago, we need at least another iteration of adjustments/improvements/updates

### Topic

#### Focused but not too niche

In general you want to choose a topic that is not too broad as you will be unable to present the content with enough depth and breadth due to time constraints and audience attention span, and clarity will be an issue. On the other hand, if you choose a topic that is too focused or very niche, it won't be relevant or interesting to the majority of your audience. Balance is the key.

#### Relevancy

When choosing a topic, you want to take into consideration two main factors — current tech trends and the audience you are presenting to.

**Tech Trends**

Ideally, you will choose a topic that is innovative, emerging, or controversial, or a topic where you bring new insight or interpretation to existing, popular technology. You can use Tikal's Tech Radar or other tools that map current trends in terms of libraries and technologies like package popularity in NPM (number of downloads), number of stars in Github, Stackoverflow yearly survey and etc.

### From subject to story

Stories are one of the most important treasures of humanity and we learn much better from a story than an assorted collection of facts. If you structure your lecture as a story, the audience will be much more focused, since they will always want to know "what happens next".

Here's a possible recipe:

To create a story, first capture the interest of the audience with a disruptive message.

This should be something that will make them wonder, will break their perception of reality as it is in their eyes, or just make them feel just a bit insecure about it.

Now, start your story.
Choose a hero - it can be you, or any other person, or just a made up character.
The hero of your story should encounter a problem. The problem should be something important and should of course be connected to the subject of the lecture.
The naive solution - our hero tries a simple way to solve the problem and fails (the story may include multiple different "tries" and "failures").

The inspiration - our hero understands the flaws of the naive solution/s or gets inspired by another thing that leads to the right solution.

The solution - our hero finds the right solution for the problem and implements it. The solution is correct, the problem is solved and our hero wins!

The aftermath - revisit the disruptive message and see how our hero's tale affected it.

### Slides

Tip: the slides are supporting the talk, they are NOT the talk. Start by planning the talk as if there are no slides at all, only after it's formed, start thinking about sides

When creating the slides, have the following in mind:

- Limit the amount of text/bullet-points, keep in mind that people understand better when information is presented in small chunks
- A picture worths a thousand words
- A picture worths even more if it's funny/interesting/intriguing
- Use high resolution images
- Be consistent with the fonts, headers, titles etc (don't use different font size for a title in different slides for example)
- Our mind can count up to 6 objects instantly. 7 and up will cause a delay. Keep the number of objects in your slides up to 6. Usually we don't need a slide number and company logo in every slide.
- Keep the rest of your notes in the "speaker notes" area (which are not shown to the audience)
- Our attention is usually directed at high contrast, bright displays. This is why TV and computer screens cause us to be dazzled. If you want the audience to pay attention to you, rather than the slides, set the background to a dark one. Use bright text
- Tip: if you want to have all the focus on you in a certain point: it's perfectly fine to insert a "blank" slide!
- 10-20-30 rule: Set a good pace, such as 10 slides should take 20 minutes with font 30.

### Code Display

Live coding requires a lot of concentration and skill, making it very hard to execute during a lecture.

Even if you're feeling very confident, make sure you have the code in slides or a different alternative as "plan B".

Typing (live coding or demoing) and talking simultaneously is hard. If you plan to type a lot, Consider using a "pilot" to do the typing for you. If you do use a "pilot" — reherse.

Displaying code in slides is challenging because it can easily fill out the entire slide and make the audience lose focus. In order to make sure the audience's attention is on the portion of code you're discussing, there are a few techniques to guide their attention there:

- Use animation in order to focus the attention on a specific line/s of code
- Color/highlight the relevant part of the code
- Use a laser pointer

The second option may be tricky in case you want to discuss different parts of the code. One option is to "duplicate" the same code over multiple slides, and in each slide highlight the portion you intend to talk about in the order you're planning for the presentation.

## Lecturing Remotely

Keep in mind that lecturing remotely (over Zoom, for example) is significantly more challenging than lecturing in person, and requires you to adapt.

### Key challenges

- Equipment setup — camera, microphone, and monitor position
- Low audience attention span

### How to overcome the situation

- Having 2 monitors is a must. You need to be able to see your audience and your presentation slides / code. This is not a recommendation, it is a must.
- Make sure there is no feedback or echo and that your sound is clear. You don't necessarily need a fancy microphone as long as it meets this criteria.
- Place the camera in front of you so your audience feels as if you are speaking to them (not to the side of your face).
- Build into your lecture interactive elements with the audience in order to keep them engaged.

## The Audience

Make sure you know your audience and adjust your lecture according to their level of understanding.

**Level of seniority**

Verify prerequisites (e.g. the audience should know docker in order to understand a talk about Kubernetes)

**Expertise in the topic field**

For example, you would lecture about Kubernetes differently to frontend and backend developers.

Be more loyal to the audience than to the content: while during the preparations phase we should make sure to accommodate our audience skills and needs, we should also be ready to make additional adjustments "online" while we give the presentation: for example, if a certain section was built for a "rookie level" and we find out during the lecture that are audience is more advanced than we anticipated, we should skip that section and move on to the next one.

## ADDENDUM

### Frontal vs Remote

When you lecture remotely‎, the circumstances change. The focus of the audience is different and there are different tools to handle the situation. Consider the following:

- **Camera positioning** — keep your laptop/ camera elevated enough, so it will record you from above, not below. People usually look better when the camera looks slightly down to them, rather than the other way around. Sit, don't stand up — so you can be close to the camera. Use your hands to explain things.
- **Constantly engage the audience** — ask them questions and let them answer. Let them be active participants of the lecture. This will keep them from wandering away from the screen and turning off their cameras.
- Encourage them to turn on the cameras. A person with a camera on will feel more obligated to the lecture.
- When you need the audience to code for a considerable amount of time, or create a discussion on several topics in a short time — consider splitting them into rooms, allocating each room with a task/ discussion topic.
- **Background** — use a virtual background, or make sure what's behind you doesn't steal the spotlight from you when you lecture.
- **Notifications** — make sure your notifications are turned off, especially private channels such as whatsapp. A popping notification about a private matter in the middle of a presentation can be quite an uncomfortable experience and also it might keep distracting the audience.

If the lecture was originally designed as a remote lecture and then it becomes a frontal lecture (or vice versa), make sure you're prepared for this change and rehearse both live and remote situations.

### Workshops

#### Checkup

It's expected from the LA (lecture advisor) to check (live with the lecturer) the exercises (content-wise, and ask if the lecturer checked that everything is working).

In a workshop it could be beneficial (if possible) to have a few different people reviewing the workshop and providing their input:

- Are the topics explained in clarity?
- Are there any additional resources required?
- Are the exercises clear?
- Are they too easy/difficult?
- How long does it take to go through the entire workshop?

The lecturer should account for different environments and platforms.

#### Preparation

Make sure the audience comes prepared, you might want to share the following instructions prior to the lecture:

- If they need to read something, pre-install packages/IDE, set up an environment etc.
- Write an instructions document and attach it to the invitation email.
- You can add a "self-check" to verify things were installed correctly (such as --version to verify the version installed is the required one).
- Make sure to send more than one reminder, the last reminder should be sent one day before the workshop.

#### Approach

(There are 2 main approaches, with a range between them):

**Practical / Challenges** — The practical approach sweeps through most of the toolset, emphasizes on site problem solving (during the workshop), and teaches less theory.

The idea is that the audience can learn the subject better by solving problems related to it. Usually, problems they will incur in real life.

**Theoretical / Walkthrough** — The theoretical (academic) approach strives to provide the design/architecture of the technology. It discusses the motivation for the decisions about how its components are connected and how they communicate. It also teaches a subset of the tools in a very light manner. This approach doesn't aim to make the engineer "production ready" to use the technology but rather provide the approach and ability to understand the technicalities, once they are met, with a deeper understanding behind of the how/why it works or should work in a certain way. Step by step worksheet for "self learning" with lecturer assisting.

In short: Solving the problem (Practical) vs getting to know the tool (Theoretical).

#### Structure

**Lecture**

- Theoretical approach will invest more time here.
- In the theoretical approach, the audience will learn the matter at hand in an in depth manner.
- In the practical approach, the audience will learn the bare bones and background of the matter, in order to enable them to solve the challenges ahead.

**Hands-on**

- Practical approach will invest more time here
- In the practical approach, the audience will be solving challenging problems in order to learn.
- In the theoretical approach, the audience will be walked through step by step in order to understand the way to do it.

The lecture and the hands-on can be intertwined, some of this and some of that.

#### Expectations & Exercises

A workshop usually creates the expectation of "hands on" which means that people expect it to be practical as well. We should communicate our chosen approach (theoretical/ practical) to our audience to align expectations.

If possible, split the audience into smaller groups (in zoom we can divide into rooms). Doing this increases the visibility and impact of every participant.

Make sure to "build up" the exercises, start easy and gradually increase the level

If you chose the practical approach:

- It's okay to provide the solutions for the first 2-3 items but when we're getting to the harder tasks we should try to avoid spoon-feeding.
- Provide the answers as close as possible to the end of the session or in a follow-up email.
- In case the workshop is built "one step on top of the other" (meaning, we can't continue to the next step if we're stuck), you can create different branches: one just with the questions and another with the solutions. Then they can move to the next step by checking out the branch that contains the solutions and build on top of it.
- Try to make the exercise as much as possible to "real world problems" with even small but complete examples. The audience will be able to take that example and use it as a reference.
- You can use tools like codelab.

### Definition of quality per rank of lecture

- **Lightning talk** — this is the most casual level of a lecture. We usually won't assist at this phase, as the lecture is short and presented in a very "raw" state.
- **Group lecture** — this is the standard Tikal inner lecture. Usually 1 lecture advisor session is sufficient here. More are possible if both the lecturer and the advisor are interested. The lecture should be of good level, answering most of the ideas in this handbook, but it doesn't have to be polished to the highest level.
- **Meetup** — this lecture is presented to an outside audience, representing Tikal in front of the community. Therefore, it must be polished to a higher level, and it should be reviewed prior to the date by with least 1 advisor session. More than 1 is highly recommended.
- **Convention** — The highest level of lecture, presented to a large outer audience. This means the lecture should be in pristine condition. Multiple sessions with an advisor are mandatory to achieve the quality needed. Advisors should do the cycles until it's good for final review from Lior.
